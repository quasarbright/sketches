Differential Growth, in 3D

The 2D version is a chain of beads on a string, refusing to touch itself in a bounded dish. This is the same idea lifted into a volume: either a surface — a mesh of vertices stitched into triangles, like a growing membrane — or a line — several closed loops of points, each its own color, like separate growing tubes. Every step, every point feels the same few rules — and, in line mode, competes for space with every other curve too.

stay connected

Each point is pulled toward its mesh or chain neighbors so edges hold a target length. On the surface, it's also nudged toward the average position of its neighbors — smoothing relaxes sharp creases into gentler, more coral-like folds.

personal space

Any two points that drift too close — even ones far apart along the mesh or chain — push each other away. This is what keeps the growing form from passing through itself.

growth

Whenever an edge stretches past a length limit, it splits: a new point is born at its midpoint. On the surface, splitting an edge also splits the one or two triangles that share it. The form is constantly gaining points and surface area.

the enclosure

All of this happens inside an invisible sphere — cross its radius and you're pulled back in. A surface that must hold ever more area inside a fixed volume has only one option: buckle and wrinkle, the same reason a folded cortex fits more brain inside a fixed skull. Nobody programs the folds; they're forced by growing area + no self-overlap + a fixed enclosure.

why it looks like this

A point sparks in its colony's complementary color the instant it's born from a split, then cools within a couple dozen steps into the colony's own vivid blue — a spark that contrasts against any color the colony could be, not a fixed color that would vanish against a same-colored colony.
