Colony

the real thing

A petri dish is a shallow round dish filled with agar — a clear gel with food mixed into it. Smear a drop of bacteria onto it and, over the next hours or days, it eats the food, multiplies, and spreads into a visible fuzzy blob: a colony. Depending on how much food is around and how it's mixed in, real colonies grow into solid round discs, frost-like branching patterns, concentric rings, or curling pinwheels — all from the same bacteria, just different conditions.

how the computer fakes this

It doesn't simulate individual bacteria — a real colony has billions. Instead the screen is divided into a grid of squares, one per pixel, like graph paper laid over the dish. (To avoid confusion with an actual living bacterium, this page calls these grid squares, never "cells.")

Every grid square tracks three things: how much food is left in that patch of agar, how much colony has grown there (0 = bare agar, up to full), and how strong a scent trail is sitting on it — a chemical that growing colony releases, which pulls other growth toward it.

Each tick: food and scent both flow a little into neighboring squares, the way a drop of dye spreads through water. Any square with colony on it eats a bit of its own food and adds a bit of scent. Any empty square next to the colony has a chance to get colonized next, faster if there's food left in it and faster still if a strong scent trail is coming from next door.

why it branches

When food is scarce, a square that gets colonized eats up what little food its immediate neighbors had too, so they can't grow anymore. Growth can then only continue by racing outward at a few lucky, still-fed points — each one racing ahead while the gaps between stay bare. That's the branching, frost-like shape.

Turn food level and food diffusion (how fast food flows back into eaten patches) up, and no patch stays starved for long, so the whole growing edge advances evenly instead — a solid, compact disc.

Bacterial spreading works against that same branching, from a different direction: it lets the bacteria themselves leak a little into empty neighboring squares on their own, the way gas spreads through a room, regardless of food or crowding. Since that's a smoothing effect rather than a starvation-driven one, turning it up fattens and blurs sharp branches back toward a solid shape even while food stays scarce.

the other knobs

Chemotaxis makes growth prefer squares with a strong scent trail, which pulls separate branches back toward each other instead of letting them spread apart.

Chirality adds a small, consistent bias toward turning one direction as a branch extends — the same asymmetry that makes real colonies of some E. coli strains curl into pinwheels instead of growing straight out.

Ring period pulses the whole colony's growth rate on a repeating timer instead of letting it grow continuously — real colonies do this too, synchronizing bursts of growth through chemical signaling, which leaves rings behind the way tree rings mark a season.

the name

The specimen label in the corner isn't decoration — it's read directly off whichever knobs are currently dominant, the way a field microbiologist would tentatively name an unfamiliar isolate by its colony's shape.
