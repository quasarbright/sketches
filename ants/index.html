Ants & Pheromones

No ant here knows where the food is, or that a shortest path even exists. Each one just follows two simple rules, and the shortest route between nest and food emerges anyway — stigmergy: coordination through traces left in a shared environment, rather than through communication or planning.

SECTION — TWO PHEROMONES

  • to-food (teal): laid by ants carrying food back, marking the route they took. Ants still searching follow rising to-food concentration — it points toward food.
  • to-nest (amber): laid by ants out searching, marking their way out. Ants carrying food follow rising to-nest concentration to get home.

Wherever both trails have built up along the same route, the colors add into a bright white-gold glow — that glow is the shortest path the colony has converged on, drawn by the chemistry itself.

SECTION — WHY SHORTER PATHS WIN

Every tick, pheromone evaporates a little: $\,p_{t+1} = p_t(1-\varepsilon)$. A short path gets walked, reinforced, and re-walked faster than a long one, so its trail decays less between reinforcements and pulls ahead — the same positive-feedback-plus-evaporation loop behind real ant colony optimization algorithms for problems like the travelling salesman.

SECTION — TRY THIS

Drop a second food source and watch the colony split its effort, then let one run out and see traffic drain off that trail. Draw a wall across an established route mid-flow and watch the glow fracture and reroute around it in real time.
